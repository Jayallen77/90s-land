Mosaic gives the web a friendlier face, CERN opens the code, Jurassic Park turns CGI into public astonishment, The X-Files makes paranoia cool, and by December Doom makes the family PC feel dangerous.

Why this year matters

1993 is when screens stop feeling like separate hobbies and start acting like connected worlds. The web becomes easier to see, games get moodier, CGI gets believable, and late-night TV starts whispering that something weird is always happening just offscreen.

It is a threshold year: still full of mall rituals, magazine previews, and school-lab friction, but suddenly loaded with portals — browser windows, point-and-click islands, Fox paranoia, and shareware disks passed hand to hand.

APR

Mosaic 1.0 lands, then CERN frees the web

NCSA Mosaic 1.0 ships on April 21, and on April 30 CERN announces that the World Wide Web will be free to anyone with no fees due. That is the month the web stops feeling like a lab artifact and starts becoming public infrastructure.
